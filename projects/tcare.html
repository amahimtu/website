1.
Research.


Foundational User Experience Research.

Based on conversations with our peers, my team and I felt that accessing healthcare is a problem for many University of Toronto (U of T) students, so we decided to investigate this issue to see where the problems lie. To do so, we interviewed and surveyed U of T students on their experiences with Health & Wellness services at the university.



Affinity Diagram.

I led an affinity diagramming session, wherein we used a "space saturate and group approach", using inductive reasoning to fill a whiteboard with sticky notes of insights from user interviews and surveys. We noticed patterns emerging and formed ‘clusters’ of user insights and problems.



After much back and forth, we noticed three key clusters of user pain points:

  • New patient/first-time booking issues
  • Long wait times
  • Lack of insurance coverage info



2.
Analysis.


Persona.

Based on a careful analysis of our user interviews and affinity diagram, we developed a primary persona: Gabby the Globetrotter. Although the scope of our problem applies to both domestic and international students, the latter face more difficulty accessing Health and Wellness services at U of T since they generally lack family doctors in Canada and may be unfamiliar with the country’s healthcare systems.



Empathy Map.

To put ourselves in Gabby's shoes, we created an empathy map of what Gabby would say say, think, feel, and do, when it comes to accessing Health & Wellness services at the university. We used real data such as quotes from user interviews to inform the empathy map.



Scenario Mapping.

Building off of the empathy map, we mapped Gabby’s scenario "as-is", identifying the steps she needs to take to book an appointment at the Health & Wellness Clinic, and the actions, thoughts, and feelings that accompany them. Next, we mapped how we envisioned Gabby's scenario "to be" with our design solution. While the steps she must take remain the same, her actions, thoughts, and feelings change dramatically.

Big Ideas.

My teammmates and I spent about eight minutes ideating potential design solutions, individually sketching our ideas out on stickies before combining them on a whiteboard. We then each casted votes for ideas based on how feasible or impactful we felt the ideas were, as shown in the legend below.



One we casted our votes, we created a prioritization grid, placing the ideas where appropriate. Marginal gains represent those that had a low amount of feasibility and impact votes. Big bets have the potential for high impact, but may not be feasible in terms of resources. Quick wins are those that are highly feasible, but not necessarily impactful. Finally, home runs are ideas that are highly feasible with the potential for tremendous impact on our persona.
