Saathi: Voice-controlled Car Dashboard.

Making the driving experience in India easier, safer, and more comfortable.


Overview.

I undertook this project in a team of seven during the Monsoon Course on HCI 2021 at the prestigious IIT Bombay.

Product: A voice-controlled dashboard in cars for the Indian market. I came up with the name “Saathi” for this product, which means “companion” in Hindi, the most widely-used language in India.



Problem: Many dashboards in cars available in India are unintuitive and distracting, whether they are touchscreen-based or consist of analog buttons/knobs. These can be inconvenient and dangerous for users to use while driving (they require a considerable amount of cognitive load), especially on the characteristically chaotic roads in the country. Even where users use voice technology to assist them while driving—such as with their smartphone maps—these technologies seldom recognize Indian accents, dialects, and languages properly. Further, few dashboards in non-luxury cars are “smart” or personalized to their users.

Goal: To design a voice-controlled car dashboard that addresses the pain points and needs of users who drive regularly in India.

Client: IIT Bombay.

My Role: Product designer and project manager.

  • Facilitated two user interviews
  • Crafted revised design brief
  • Set product, business, and UX goals
  • Created personas and wrote scenarios
  • Co-created user flow
  • Crafted wireframes and prototypes for key screens
  • Spearheaded Wizard-of-Oz usability testing

Project Duration: June 2021

User Interviews.

To better understand the scope and problem areas, my team and I interviewed 10 participants living in cities across India, namely Bengaluru, Bhopal, Kerala, Kolkata, Mumbai, and New Delhi. Each individual was recruited because they regularly drive a car that either they or a family member own. The interviews were "contextual" because while they were conducted, users sat inside their cars and showed us their dashboards and driving habits. Each interview lasted 30-45 minutes.


We asked participants questions pertaining to their driving habits, current dashboard usage and what they wished their dashboard could offer, issues they faced while driving in India, and more.


Interview Data Interpretation.

We created a file containing multiple spreadsheets—one for each participant. Within each spreadsheet, we included findings from our raw notes. I then categorized each finding or comment into user statements (US), observations (OB), breakdowns (i.e., when something goes wrong for the user), insights (IN), and design ideas (DI).



Affinity Mapping.

After compiling our interview interpretations, we created an affinity map to organize the vast amount of data into themes or patterns. Using the bottom-up or “space saturate and group” approach, we included some notes from the user interviews on stickies, saturating the Miro board. We then began to recognize patterns and grouped our map into relevant clusters under summary headings. Some clusters were further grouped under even broader summary headings.

4.
Evaluation.


Usability Testing.

We needed to evaluate our designs through usability testing on real potential users. For this, we re-recruited three of the participants we interviewed in the early stages of the project: BLR1, BHP1, and BOM4. These three users closely matched many of the criteria in our personas. I observed and took notes for the first two tests, and served as the moderator for the last one (BOM4).

Before the study, I provided our users with two realistic use cases of Saathi—i.e., "scripts"—that I wrote, so that they could get a solid idea of how our product might be used in real-world scenarios.



Due to the voice-based nature of our prototype, I led a Wizard-of-Oz style usability test, with one of our team members acting as Saathi. Users would interact with him and he responded in Saathi-like fashion via voice modulation software. This ensured that there was little to no lag and gave Saathi the companion-like feel that we wanted to incorporate in it.

Users could communicate with Saathi by saying things like:

  • “Hi Saathi, let’s go on a road trip.”
  • “Hi Saathi, show me the fastest route to work.”
  • “Hi Saathi, SOS. This is an emergency.”

Saathi responded to the user verbally and the screens changed accordingly (with our teammate controlling the prototype interaction behind the scenes).


Usability Testing: Findings, Key Performance Indicators, and Analysis.

I created a key performance indicator tool to evaluate our design. Participants were requested to answer these questions immediately after the usability test. Some questions required ratings between 1 and 5 whereas some were open-ended. Each question was crafted so that users could help us refine the design of Saathi according to our revised design brief.

Reflection & Takeaways.

  • Working in a multidisciplinary team is rewarding.

    I collaborated on this project with people from different backgrounds and skill-sets. Some were already working in UX- or UX-related fields, while others weren’t. Still, we were able to successfully leverage everyone’s skills, from illustration to project management. My experience in marketing proved useful as I wrote a lot of the content, shaped many of the business aspects of Saathi, and took charge of presentations.

  • Designers' best guesses are not good enough.

    I was floored by the excellent and surprising insights our users had to offer, and we iterated our designs accordingly. Many assumptions my team made were debunked along the way. After all, users are not designers.

  • The design process is often ugly.

    On the road to the prototype, we had many raw sketches and strange ideas crop up, not to mention skirmishes within the team due to differing opinions. I learned to appreciate these aspects of the design process. These ‘ugly’ or seemingly undesirable parts of collaborative design helped us consider more possibilities and create an even better final design.

This was a challenging yet rewarding project to work on. Thank you for checking it out. I welcome feedback!
